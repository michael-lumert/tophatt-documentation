# A0 Foundations and Deck Review

## Overall Verdict

A0 now **exceeds the original plan**, in a good way.

The goal was to make A0 a true **Foundations** level: sound, spelling behavior, accents, apostrophes, liaison, gender/articles, and readiness for A1.

The A0 document says this level should make French stop feeling random, without expecting the learner to build sentences or hold conversations. The six decks now match that philosophy very closely.

I would call this A0 set **substantially complete**.

The strongest thing we achieved is that A0 now has a clear identity:

> Not “easy French sentences,” but “French orientation.”

Across the six decks, the learner gets:

- familiar-looking cognates with French sound surprises
- common non-cognate sound gaps
- accents and special characters
- elision/apostrophes
- liaison/linking
- gender/articles with visual support

That is a coherent foundation layer.

## Where We Exceeded Expectations

The pronunciation coverage is much stronger than I expected.

A0.1 and A0.2 together cover:

- nasal vowels
- French `u`
- `ou`
- `oi`
- `eu / œu`
- `eau`
- soft and hard `g`
- `ch`
- `qu`
- silent final consonants
- pronounced final consonants
- final `-er`
- final `-ez`
- `-ail`
- `-eil`
- `-eille`
- `gn`
- `s` between vowels

A0.3 is also nicely focused. It covers all nine requested items:

```text
é
è
à
ù
ê
â
ô
ç
œ
```

It does this without turning into a textbook chapter.

A0.4 and A0.5 are probably the most useful “beginner anxiety reducers.” A beginner who has seen examples like:

```text
j’ai
l’eau
c’est
s’il vous plaît
les amis
vous avez
deux ans
```

will be much less startled when French starts compressing and linking words.

A0.6 is much better than the earlier gender plan. The rhythm of:

```text
word
the word
a/an word
```

is clear, and the gender SVG on the first encounter gives a useful visual cue without requiring a grammar lecture.

The pattern hints for:

```text
-tion / -sion
-té
-age
-eau
```

are useful, especially because the deck still includes cautions for exceptions like:

```text
image
eau
```

## Main Place We Overshot

The A0 document estimated:

```text
250–400 card exposures
5–6 decks
40–80 cards per deck
```

The actual decks are roughly:

```text
A0.1 Cognates: 122
A0.2 Sound Gaps: 79
A0.3 Accents: 46
A0.4 Elision: 52
A0.5 Liaison: 60
A0.6 Gender/Articles: 100
Total: 459 cards
```

So we exceeded the original size target.

That is not automatically bad, but it is worth noticing. A0.1 and A0.6 are the two large decks.

My recommendation: **do not trim yet**.

Study them first. If they feel heavy, the likely fix is not deletion but splitting:

```text
A0.1a — Cognates: Core Sounds
A0.1b — More Cognates

A0.6a — le / la / l’
A0.6b — un / une + gender patterns
```

But I would not split until real-world use tells you there is a problem.

## Areas That May Need Later Adjustment

### 1. A0.4 and A0.5 Contain Sentence-Like Material

This is probably unavoidable.

Elision and liaison happen between words, so we need chunks like:

```text
c’est important
vous avez
ils ont
ce n’est pas facile
```

The risk is that a learner may think:

> Am I supposed to understand all this grammar?

The fix is probably a deck intro or first-card hint:

```text
You are not learning sentence grammar yet. These are sound chunks. Listen for how the words connect.
```

---

### 2. A0.6 May Be Slightly More Conceptual Than the Others

The gender deck necessarily introduces a little abstraction:

```text
masculine / feminine
le / la
un / une
l’ hiding gender
suffix clues
```

That is fine, but if it feels too much during study, the simplest fix is to add a reassuring opening hint:

```text
Do not memorize gender rules. Let the image, article, and repeated audio help the word feel familiar.
```

---

### 3. Some A0.1 Cards Have Blank Hints

Examples include:

```text
avenue
image
orange
importance
```

That is not necessarily a flaw. Not every card needs a hint. In fact, too many hints would make the deck feel like a textbook.

I would leave these alone unless a real learner gets confused.

---

### 4. App Usage Is Mostly in the Document, Not the Decks

The A0 document explains:

- replay
- speed control
- passive/autoplay
- double-tap definitions
- typing French characters

The decks themselves have some hints, but not many.

That is probably okay if the UI has dismissible hints. If not, we could later add a few recurring reminders:

```text
Replay this one three times.
Try 0.75x, then normal speed.
Double-tap a word to see more examples.
```

I would not overdo it.

---

## Possible Missing Piece

The only thing I might eventually add is a tiny **A0 landing note** that appears before the first deck, maybe not a deck itself:

```text
A0 is different from the rest of the course. You are not trying to speak yet. You are building your ear, your eyes, and your comfort with French spelling. Replay often. Move quickly when something feels easy.
```

The A0 document already says this well; the question is whether the learner will actually see it inside the app.

---

## Final Assessment

I would not call this “fallen short.”

I’d say:

> A0 now has a better pedagogical shape than the original course plan.

It is focused, audio-first, non-textbooky, and it prepares the learner for the exact things that usually make French feel confusing:

- silent letters
- accents
- apostrophes
- linked words
- gender

My practical advice:

> Freeze these decks for now and study them.

The next improvements should come from actual use, not more theoretical polishing.
