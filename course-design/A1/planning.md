# A1 French Course Planning Checkpoint

## Current Direction

A0 is now treated as **French orientation**:

- sound and spelling behavior
- accents and special characters
- elision
- liaison
- gender/articles
- comfort with French-looking and French-sounding words

A1 should now become **first real usage**.

The goal is not to memorize a word list or complete grammar units. The goal is for learners to understand and produce simple useful sentences about themselves, other people, places, needs, actions, questions, and tiny situations.

## Core A1 Philosophy

A1 should continue the course philosophy:

- sentences over isolated vocabulary
- pattern recognition over grammar explanation
- audio-first learning
- high repetition, but not sterile drilling
- useful personal language as soon as possible
- quick tips only when they reduce confusion
- compact decks instead of giant vocabulary dumps

## 1000-Word List Role

The 1000-word Lexique-derived list should be used as a **coverage map**, not as the learning method.

Bad workflow:

> Take the top 1000 words and make cards for each word.

Better workflow:

> Define useful A1 sentence loops, write natural sentences, then check which high-frequency words/chunks are covered.

The “1000 words” target should include **words and chunks**, such as:

- je suis
- j’ai
- je veux
- je peux
- j’aime
- il y a
- il faut
- est-ce que
- parce que
- s’il vous plaît
- chez
- de / du / des / de la
- y
- en

This is more honest than pretending tiny grammar-heavy words can be mastered in isolation.

## A1 Milestone

A reasonable learner-facing milestone:

> By the end of A1, learners have met, heard, and used at least 1000 high-frequency French words and chunks in real sentences.

This gives learners confidence without turning the app into Anki.

## Pattern Deck Problem

The old A1.1 Expanded Patterns deck is useful but too spreadsheet-like.

It repeats patterns such as:

- I want to eat.
- I want to drink.
- I want to work.
- I can eat.
- I can drink.
- I can work.

The repetition is pedagogically useful, but the experience can feel sterile.

The fix is **repetition with situation drift**.

Instead of:

> I want to eat.  
> I want to drink.  
> I want to work.

Prefer:

> I want to eat.  
> I want to eat now.  
> I want to drink coffee.  
> I want to go home.  
> I don’t want to work now.

Same pattern, more human.

## Personal Routine Decks

A1 should prepare learners to create personal micro-decks.

Example learner sentences:

- I don’t want to get up.
- I’m going to make coffee.
- I’m taking the dogs for a walk.
- I need to work.
- I’m tired.
- I’m going home.

This should probably become either:

- an official A1 deck, or
- a guided custom-deck builder inside the app.

Possible name:

> A1 Personal Routine Builder

## Numbers and Days

Numbers and days should be taught mostly in context, not as isolated memorization decks.

A1 examples:

- two people
- three friends
- four legs
- five minutes
- ten euros
- seven days
- monday morning
- friday evening
- saturday afternoon

A2 can fill in more complete number/date/time coverage.

## Proposed A1 Deck Types

A1 should use three deck types:

### 1. Pattern Decks

Controlled repetition of high-value sentence frames.

Examples:

- I want...
- I can...
- I need...
- I like...
- I don’t like...
- I have...
- I am...

### 2. Situation Decks

Useful real-life language.

Examples:

- ordering coffee
- asking for help
- asking where something is
- talking about work/home/family
- simple travel or city situations

### 3. Micro-Story Decks

Tiny coherent scenes with repeated vocabulary.

Examples:

- a morning routine
- going to a café
- losing keys
- meeting a friend
- walking the dog
- buying something at a market

## Tatoeba Sentence Bank Note

The 450,000-sentence Tatoeba bank may be useful, but it should not drive the curriculum directly.

Best use:

- find common sentence frames
- validate whether proposed A1 loops are natural/common
- extract example structures
- identify repeated chunks

Risky use:

- automatically generating A1 decks from raw frequency
- trusting translations blindly
- letting corpus frequency override learner usefulness

Tatoeba should be treated as a mining tool, not a course designer.
