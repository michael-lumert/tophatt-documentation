Yes. **DELF A1** is a very reasonable first external milestone, especially because it tests practical survival French rather than “knowing French grammar.” It is an official diploma for CEFR A1, and the adult version is **DELF tout public**. France Éducation international describes A1 as the level where you can introduce yourself, understand simple oral instructions, read short simple texts, fill out forms, write a short postcard/message, answer simple personal questions, ask simple questions, and handle a basic purchase/payment situation. ([France Éducation international][1])

## DELF A1 structure

The exam has **four sections**, each worth **25 points**, for a total of **100 points**. You need **50/100 overall**, and you must score at least **5/25 in every section**; a score below 5 in any one section is eliminatory. ([France Éducation international][1])

| Skill                                   |                      Time | What you do                                                           |
| --------------------------------------- | ------------------------: | --------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Listening** / Compréhension de l’oral |                   ~20 min | 4 exercises: listen to short everyday recordings and answer questions |
| **Reading** / Compréhension des écrits  |                    30 min | 4 exercises: read short everyday documents and answer questions       |
| **Writing** / Production écrite         |                    30 min | 2 exercises: fill out a form + write a message of at least 40 words   |
| **Speaking** / Production orale         | 5–7 min, with 10 min prep | 3 parts: guided interview, information exchange, simulated dialogue   |

The first three are **collective exams** taken in a room with other candidates. Speaking is an **individual exam** with two examiners. ([France Éducation international][1])

## What the speaking test feels like

The speaking exam is short, but it is probably the part you’d want to prepare most deliberately because passive exposure alone won’t fully train it.

You should be able to:

**1. Introduce yourself**

> Bonjour. Je m’appelle Michael. Je suis américain. J’habite à Fort Worth. J’apprends le français.

**2. Answer simple questions**

> Vous habitez où ?
> Vous travaillez ?
> Qu’est-ce que vous aimez faire ?
> Vous avez des enfants ?

**3. Ask simple questions**

> Vous vous appelez comment ?
> Vous habitez où ?
> Vous aimez le café ?
> Vous travaillez où ?

**4. Handle a simulated daily-life situation**

The official criteria mention situations like asking for information about a service, asking about price/color/size, paying, greeting, thanking, and saying goodbye. ([France Éducation international][2])

Example:

> Bonjour, je voudrais un café, s’il vous plaît.
> C’est combien ?
> Je peux payer par carte ?
> Merci, au revoir.

## What to study

For A1, I’d focus less on “chapters of grammar” and more on **high-frequency task scripts**.

You need enough French to do these things comfortably:

### Personal identity

Name, nationality, city, family, age, work, hobbies.

> Je m’appelle…
> J’habite à…
> Je suis américain.
> Je travaille dans…
> J’aime…

### Daily life

Food, café, store, transportation, time, days, appointments, weather, places.

> Je voudrais…
> Je cherche…
> Où est… ?
> À quelle heure… ?
> Combien ça coûte ?

### Basic social language

Greetings, politeness, agreement, confusion, requests for repetition.

> Bonjour.
> Merci.
> Excusez-moi.
> Je ne comprends pas.
> Vous pouvez répéter, s’il vous plaît ?

### Writing templates

The writing section includes a form and a short message of **at least 40 words**. The official criteria emphasize matching the task, giving required information, using simple connectors like **et** and **alors**, greeting/closing properly, choosing **tu/vous**, and using simple A1 grammar such as present tense and futur proche. ([France Éducation international][2])

A safe A1 message template:

> Salut Paul,
> Je suis à Paris. Je vais bien. Il fait beau. Aujourd’hui, je visite un musée et je mange au restaurant avec ma famille. Demain, je vais aller au parc. Je rentre dimanche.
> À bientôt,
> Michael

That is not elegant French, but it is exactly the kind of controlled, functional writing A1 rewards.

## How I’d prepare, given your learning style

Your app/deck approach is already well aligned with **listening, reading, and phrase exposure**. The missing DELF-specific piece is **active output under constraints**.

I’d prepare in three tracks:

### 1. Keep using sentence/audio decks daily

Use your current approach for automatic recognition: cognates, sound gaps, articles, liaison, elision, basic A1 phrases. For DELF, though, make sure you are not only recognizing sentences. You need to be able to **produce** a small number of useful scripts.

### 2. Build a tiny “DELF A1 survival deck”

Not a giant curriculum deck. More like 80–120 cards focused on exam tasks:

| Category               | Example target                   |
| ---------------------- | -------------------------------- |
| Introduce yourself     | Je m’appelle Michael.            |
| Ask personal questions | Vous habitez où ?                |
| Café/store             | Je voudrais un café.             |
| Price/payment          | C’est combien ?                  |
| Directions             | Où est la gare ?                 |
| Time/date              | Le rendez-vous est à dix heures. |
| Writing closings       | À bientôt / Cordialement         |
| Repair phrases         | Vous pouvez répéter ?            |

I would make these **two-way**: English → French for production, and French → English for comprehension.

### 3. Practice the actual exam format early

France Éducation international provides official A1 sample papers, audio, correction documents, speaking documents, and evaluation grids. Their sample page also notes that two official formats exist for listening/reading, but neither format gives an advantage or disadvantage. ([France Éducation international][2])

Do one official sample as a diagnostic before you feel ready. Don’t wait until the end. Your first score will tell you whether your weak area is listening, reading, writing, or speaking.

## Practical study plan

For an A0-ish learner aiming at A1, I’d think in terms of **8–12 weeks**, depending on how much active speaking/writing you do.

**Weeks 1–2: A1 survival phrases**

Daily:

* 15–20 min listening/reading deck work
* 10 min speaking aloud
* 5 min copying/writing simple sentences

Goal: introduce yourself, ask/answer basic questions, buy something, ask price/time/place.

**Weeks 3–4: DELF task practice**

Daily:

* 1 listening exercise or short audio
* 1 tiny reading document: ad, schedule, invitation, email
* 3–5 spoken answers aloud

Twice per week:

* Write one 40–60 word message.

**Weeks 5–6: Mock exam rhythm**

Do timed practice:

* Listening: no pausing except where the exam allows repetition
* Reading: answer without translating everything
* Writing: form + 40-word message in 30 minutes
* Speaking: record yourself doing a 5-minute oral

**Weeks 7–8+: Polish weak points**

If listening is weak: repeat official-style audio and train numbers, times, prices, dates.
If speaking is weak: rehearse the same 20 personal answers until they are automatic.
If writing is weak: memorize 5 reusable message frames.
If reading is weak: practice schedules, signs, menus, ads, short emails.

## What I’d prioritize most

For passing A1, the highest return items are:

1. **Numbers, prices, times, dates** — these show up everywhere.
2. **Question words** — où, quand, combien, comment, pourquoi, qui, quel.
3. **Politeness scripts** — bonjour, s’il vous plaît, merci, au revoir.
4. **Present tense of common verbs** — être, avoir, aller, faire, aimer, vouloir, pouvoir.
5. **Futur proche** — je vais visiter, je vais aller, je vais acheter.
6. **A tiny speaking autobiography** — 8–10 sentences about yourself.
7. **A reusable 40-word message template**.

You don’t need to be impressive. You need to be **clear, simple, and task-complete**.
