# Course Blueprint — General

_Status: reorganized June 2026_  
_Scope: language-neutral course strategy from A0 through B2._

This document owns the overall course philosophy and progression model. Level-specific implementation details belong in level blueprints, such as `A0-Blueprint-General.md`. Language-specific implementation details belong in language adapters, such as `A0-Adapter-French.md` and `A0-Adapter-Norwegian.md`.

---

# Big Philosophy

This course is designed for fast, practical language acquisition using:

- audio-first exposure
- useful words, chunks, phrases, and sentences
- pattern recognition instead of grammar memorization
- high repetition early and wider variation later
- real-world usage as soon as possible
- minimal explanation, used only when it reduces confusion

The goal is not to build a textbook. The goal is to build a progression engine that helps learners recognize, understand, and eventually produce useful language.

A learner should not feel that they are memorizing tables. They should feel that the language is becoming less strange, more predictable, and more usable.

# Core Learning Principles

## 1. Audio Comes First

Every level should support listening, replay, slower audio, and repetition. The app should make it easy to:

- listen at very slow speed: `0.50x`
- listen at slow speed: `0.75x`
- listen at normal speed: `1.0x`
- slightly increase speed: `1.25x`
- replay the target-language audio without penalty
- use passive or hands-free listening after initial exposure

Audio is not a decoration. It is one of the main learning channels.

## 2. Pattern Before Explanation

The course should teach patterns by showing them repeatedly in useful contexts.

Good:

```text
I have the key.
Do you have the key?
I need the key.
I found the key.
```

Avoid:

```text
Here is a grammar table explaining every form before the learner has heard the pattern.
```

## 3. Useful Language Before Complete Language

Early decks should not try to cover every rule, exception, or form. They should give the learner useful orientation and high-value material.

Completeness is not the goal. Momentum is the goal.

## 4. Short Explanations Only When Helpful

Quick Tips are allowed across the course, but they should be brief, practical, and easy to ignore if the learner already understands.

Good:

```text
After “I want,” the next verb often stays in its basic form.
```

Avoid:

```text
This is an infinitive complement governed by a semi-auxiliary modal construction.
```

## 5. Repetition Is Designed, Not Accidental

Important words, chunks, sounds, and sentence frames should appear multiple times. Repetition is especially important early.

Repetition becomes bloat when the learner is doing the same job with the same item again and again. Repetition is useful when the learner has a new job to do with familiar material.

# Course Progression

## A0 — Orientation / Bootstrapping

A0 is not the first conversation level. A0 prepares the learner for sentence-building.

By the end of A0, the learner should feel that the language no longer looks or sounds random. They should recognize common sounds, spelling behavior, tiny words/chunks, and app audio tools.

A0 implementation rules live in `A0-Blueprint-General.md`.

## A1 — First Real Usage

A1 is where the learner begins true sentence-building and tiny conversations.

By the end of A1, the learner should be able to understand and use simple predictable patterns, common questions, everyday phrases, and basic interactions.

A1 should introduce real usage while still keeping grammar load low.

Likely A1 deck families:

- expanded core patterns
- daily-life situations
- question and answer patterns
- tiny conversations
- polite questions
- family, places, time, people, and common actions
- connectors and simple negation

## A2 — Sentence Builder

A2 expands the learner from isolated short sentences into connected everyday usage.

By the end of A2, the learner should handle ordinary daily-life situations, understand short connected speech, express simple opinions, describe needs and plans, and recognize common past/future patterns.

A2 is a strong place for vocabulary expansion through familiar grammar.

Good A2 vocabulary expansion:

```text
I lost the key.
I found the key.
Do you have the key?
I need the key.
```

Avoid adding new vocabulary, new grammar, and idioms all at once.

## B1 — Independence

B1 shifts from sentence mastery toward paragraph and story comprehension.

The learner should begin to follow connected scenes, tell simple stories, explain preferences, and handle ordinary situations with more flexibility.

B1 decks may still use short cards, but those cards can belong to longer connected scenes.

## B2 — Fluency Builder

B2 moves toward denser listening, native-like phrasing, longer stories, more nuance, and learner-directed content.

At B2, exposure becomes much heavier. The course should help the learner bridge from controlled material into real content.

# Deck Families

Not every level needs every deck type, but the course can draw from these families.

## 1. Pattern Decks

Controlled sentences with high repetition.

Purpose: build intuition for common frames.

## 2. Usage Decks

Real-life sentences with practical variation.

Purpose: connect patterns to situations.

## 3. Interaction Decks

Simple back-and-forth language.

Purpose: prepare the learner for conversation rhythm.

## 4. Vocabulary Expansion Decks

New useful vocabulary introduced through familiar structures.

Purpose: expand breadth without bloating grammar load.

## 5. Micro-Story Decks

Short connected sequences around one small event.

Purpose: bridge from cards to stories.

## 6. Exposure Decks

Longer input, adapted stories, natural dialogues, and eventually native-like content.

Purpose: transition to real-world comprehension.

# Quick Tips Layer

Quick Tips are a permanent support layer across the course.

Rules:

- 1–2 sentences when possible
- plain language
- no memorization required
- added only at confusion points
- directly connected to the current card
- not repeated unless useful

Quick Tips should help the learner return to listening and noticing. They should not become miniature textbook chapters.

---

# Grammar Approach

Grammar is not taught as a separate subject in the main learning flow.

Instead:

- patterns appear repeatedly
- learners notice similarities
- Quick Tips reduce confusion
- formal labels are avoided unless genuinely helpful
- production is delayed until the learner has heard enough examples

This does not mean grammar is ignored. It means grammar is absorbed through repeated, meaningful exposure before it is named.

---

# Analyzer and Count Philosophy

Counts are diagnostic, not pass/fail metrics.

Useful metrics include:

- card count
- word/chunk count
- sentence count
- repeated words/chunks
- words appearing only once
- words appearing 2–3 times
- words appearing 4+ times
- unusually long cards
- exact duplicate targets

Counts should raise questions, not automatically decide answers.

A repeated word may be bloat, or it may be a useful anchor. A long card may be too hard, or it may be a formulaic phrase that works well in audio.

---

# Personalization System

Learners should eventually be able to create personal decks around:

- family
- work
- travel
- hobbies
- routines
- common errands
- upcoming trips or events

Personalized decks should follow the same principles:

- short useful language
- natural phrasing
- audio-friendly text
- familiar grammar when adding new vocabulary
- Quick Tips only when helpful
