# TopHatt Brand Style Guide

*Working draft v0.1*

## 1. Brand Summary

**TopHatt** is a language-learning app for serious beginners who want to hear, recognize, and eventually use real language without being buried in grammar charts or isolated word memorization.

The app is built around a simple belief:

> Learners improve fastest when they meet useful words in meaningful sentences, hear them repeatedly, and gradually notice patterns for themselves.

TopHatt should feel calm, practical, and encouraging. It should respect the learner’s intelligence without assuming prior grammar knowledge. It should help people move from “I recognize a few words” to “I can hear and understand useful language in context.”

TopHatt is not a toy.
TopHatt is not a quiz machine.
TopHatt is not a grammar textbook.
TopHatt is guided exposure.

## 2. Brand Positioning

### Core promise

**TopHatt helps beginners build a real ear for a language through sentence-first, audio-rich learning materials.**

### Simple positioning statement

For motivated language learners who want more than memorization, TopHatt provides structured sentence decks, natural audio practice, and gentle pattern exposure so learners can build understanding through repeated contact with real language.

### What makes TopHatt different

TopHatt is designed around:

* **Sentences over isolated words**
* **Audio exposure before explanation overload**
* **Patterns before grammar tables**
* **High-frequency language before novelty**
* **Simple repetition without gimmicks**
* **Human-curated learning materials**
* **Comfortable mobile use, including hands-free review**

TopHatt should feel like a well-designed practice tool, not a game begging for attention.

## 3. Target Learner

TopHatt is for learners who say things like:

> “I don’t want to just memorize flashcards. I want the language to start sounding familiar.”

They may be absolute beginners, returning learners, self-learners, commuters, spouses learning together, or people who previously bounced off apps like Anki or Duolingo.

They are not necessarily language nerds. They may not know terms like “elision,” “liaison,” “definite article,” or “preterite.” When those terms are useful, TopHatt explains them lightly and immediately connects them to examples.

### Learner mindset

The learner wants:

* To understand spoken language better.
* To get used to real sounds and spelling patterns.
* To feel steady progress.
* To avoid being overwhelmed.
* To practice in short, repeatable sessions.
* To trust that the material is curated and purposeful.

The learner does **not** want:

* A noisy reward system.
* Cute but unhelpful sentences.
* Dense grammar explanations.
* Giant vocabulary dumps.
* Ambiguous icons or mystery controls.
* Flashcards that feel disconnected from actual language.

## 4. Brand Personality

TopHatt should sound and feel:

**Clear**
Plain language. No academic fog.

**Encouraging**
Supportive, but not artificially cheerful.

**Practical**
Every feature and sentence should have a reason.

**Respectful**
The learner is an adult. Do not talk down to them.

**Calm**
The app should reduce cognitive load, not create excitement for its own sake.

**Curious**
The brand can invite noticing: “Listen for this sound,” “Notice this pattern,” “You’ll see this often.”

**Human**
Content should feel hand-built, not machine-generated at scale.

## 5. Voice and Tone

### Default tone

TopHatt’s voice should be warm, concise, and plainspoken.

It should feel like a patient teacher sitting next to the learner, saying:

> “Don’t worry about mastering this yet. Just listen for the pattern.”

### Avoid

Avoid language that sounds:

* Overhyped: “Master French instantly!”
* Childish: “Great job, superstar!”
* Corporate: “Optimize your multilingual acquisition journey.”
* Punitive: “Wrong. Try again.”
* Too academic: “The grapheme corresponds to the phonemic realization...”
* Too vague: “Improve your skills with engaging content.”

### Prefer

Use direct, reassuring language:

* “Listen first.”
* “You don’t need to memorize this yet.”
* “This spelling is normal in French.”
* “The final sound is still clear.”
* “You’ll meet this pattern again.”
* “Replay as much as you like.”
* “The goal is recognition, not perfection.”

## 6. Copy Principles

### Use short explanations

Hints should be brief enough to ignore but useful enough to reduce confusion.

Good:

> `é` sounds like a short, clean “ay.”

Too much:

> The acute accent indicates a close-mid front unrounded vowel, historically derived from...

### Explain only what helps now

A beginner card should not become a grammar lesson. If a note does not help the learner understand the current sentence, save it for later.

Good:

> Feminine form. A male speaker would write `désolé`.

Too much:

> French adjectives agree in gender and number with the noun or implied subject...

### Prefer “notice” language

TopHatt should teach learners to observe patterns.

Good:

> Notice the article: `une école`.

Good:

> Listen for the link between the words.

Good:

> The last `e` is not pronounced separately.

### Be honest about difficulty

TopHatt should normalize confusion.

Good:

> This may sound fast at first. Replay it a few times.

Good:

> You do not need to produce this perfectly yet.

## 8. Visual Identity

TopHatt should look calm, readable, and gently polished. It should not look like a children’s game, a corporate LMS, or a raw spreadsheet.

The visual style should support:

* Fast recognition
* Comfortable mobile use
* Large readable text
* Clear primary actions
* Minimal distraction
* Audio-focused study

### Visual keywords

**Soft**
Rounded corners, gentle contrast, no harsh edges.

**Readable**
Large text, strong spacing, short line lengths.

**Warm**
Colors should feel human and inviting, not sterile.

**Structured**
Decks, paths, modes, and cards should feel organized.

**Quietly distinctive**
The app can have personality, but the learning material should remain the star.

## 9. Color Direction

TopHatt’s colors should be calm and functional.

### Recommended color roles

**Primary / brand color**
Warm gold, tan, muted amber, or another refined “TopHatt” accent. This color can carry brand identity.

**Success / suggested action**
Green should be reserved for recommended next actions. Do not use green casually if it also means “suggested.”

**Gender support colors**
Soft pink and soft blue may be used for gender illustrations, but they should remain gentle and readable.

**Backgrounds**
Prefer soft neutrals over pure white when possible. Reading and listening should feel comfortable for long sessions.

**Dark / low-light mode**
Dark mode should feel calm, not neon. It is especially important for evening review and hands-free use.

### Avoid

* Bright gamified palettes
* Too many competing accent colors
* Low-contrast text
* Red/green dependency without labels
* Hardcoded one-off colors that break theme consistency

## 10. Typography

Typography should feel clear, modern, and unpretentious.

### Principles

Use type to create hierarchy, not decoration.

The learner should immediately understand:

1. What sentence they are studying.
2. What language they are hearing.
3. What action they can take next.
4. Whether any hint is available.

### Text behavior

* Target-language text should be large and highly readable.
* Hints should be smaller but not tiny.
* Avoid dense paragraphs inside flashcard views.
* Use bold sparingly for key labels.
* Use code-style formatting for exact letters, spellings, and phrases, such as `é`, `l’`, or `désolée`.

## 11. UI Principles

TopHatt’s interface should feel like a tool designed by someone who has actually studied with it.

### One primary action

Each screen should make the next useful action obvious.

Examples:

* Continue learning
* Replay audio
* Show answer
* Pause hands-free review
* Move to next card

### Comfortable touch targets

Controls should be large enough for real mobile use. Avoid tiny icons, cramped rows, or thumb-unfriendly layouts.

### Clear modes

Mode names should be visible and understandable. Avoid making the learner infer behavior from icons alone.

Good:

> Echo — Listen first, then reveal.

Less good:

> Recognition Mode

### Avoid surprise

Do not make the layout jump unnecessarily. Do not hide major actions. Do not change behavior without showing the learner what changed.

### Audio-first design

Because audio is central to TopHatt, replay should always be easy. Listening should never feel like a secondary feature.

## 12. Content Principles

TopHatt content should be curated, not merely generated.

### Sentence-first

Words are useful, but sentences teach sound, rhythm, grammar, and meaning together.

A good sequence might be:

> café
> un café
> Je voudrais un café.
> Un autre café, s’il vous plaît.

### Use familiar anchors

Beginners benefit from cognates and recognizable words early. These build confidence while introducing the sound system.

### Teach patterns gently

TopHatt can teach concepts like accents, elision, liaison, articles, and gender, but only through small, repeated encounters.

### Keep beginner content normal

Avoid bizarre sentences unless there is a strong teaching reason. The learner should feel they are meeting language they might actually hear or use.

### Frequency matters

Prioritize common words, useful chunks, and everyday phrasing. Rare vocabulary can wait.

### Context beats explanation

A short phrase often teaches more than a long note.

Good:

> bonne idée !

This teaches `idée`, final `ée`, adjective order, and a useful reaction — without needing to explain all of that at once.

## 13. Naming Guidance

**TopHatt** currently suggests something slightly classic, distinctive, and memorable. It has personality without being childish. However, if the name changes, the replacement should preserve the same core feeling:

* Smart but not academic
* Friendly but not cute
* Memorable but not gimmicky
* Broad enough for multiple languages
* Compatible with serious learning

### Possible naming directions

**Sound / listening direction**

Names that suggest hearing, echoing, rhythm, or familiarity.

**Path / journey direction**

Names that suggest steady progress without sounding like a game.

**Craft / curation direction**

Names that suggest carefully built learning materials.

**Companion / guide direction**

Names that suggest help without sounding like a tutor mascot.

### Avoid names that imply

* Instant fluency
* AI magic
* School homework
* Child-focused learning
* Pure flashcard memorization
* Travel phrasebook only

## 14. Brand Do / Don’t

### Do

Say:

> Build an ear for French.

Don’t say:

> Master French fast with addictive flashcards.

### Do

Say:

> Listen for the `é` sound.

Don’t say:

> Learn the acute accent rule.

### Do

Say:

> You’ll meet this pattern again.

Don’t say:

> Memorize this exception.

### Do

Say:

> Practice in short sessions or hands-free.

Don’t say:

> Complete your daily challenge to maintain your streak.

### Do

Say:

> Real sentences. Clear audio. Gentle repetition.

Don’t say:

> Fun mini-games that make learning effortless.

## 15. Product Values

### Clarity over cleverness

A clear button is better than a clever icon.

### Repetition without shame

Replaying is learning. Repetition should feel normal, not remedial.

### Progress without pressure

The app should encourage consistency without manufacturing guilt.

### Real language early

Even beginners can meet real phrases if the material is carefully chosen.

### Explanations at the point of need

Teach the smallest useful thing at the moment it helps.

### Learner control

The learner should be able to browse, repeat, slow down, listen hands-free, or move on.

## 16. Brand Essence

TopHatt is a calm, sentence-first language practice app that helps learners build recognition through sound, repetition, and carefully chosen examples.

It should feel like:

> A thoughtful stack of flashcards became a listening course.

Or more simply:

> Real language, repeated until it starts to sound familiar.
