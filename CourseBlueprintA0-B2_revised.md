# Language Learning Course Blueprint (A0 → B2)

_Last updated: May 2026_

## Philosophy

This course is designed for **fast, practical language acquisition** using:

- Sentences over isolated vocabulary
- Pattern recognition over grammar memorization
- High repetition early, variation later
- Real-world usage as soon as possible
- Audio-first learning with easy replay and shadowing

No grammar study is required. Learners absorb structure naturally through exposure, with short inline explanations only when they reduce confusion.

The goal is not to build a textbook. The goal is to build a **progression engine** that helps learners recognize, understand, and eventually produce useful language.

---

# Audio & Playback System

Learners must be able to:

- Play very slow audio: **0.50x**
- Play slow audio: **0.75x**
- Play normal audio: **1.0x**
- Slightly increase speed: **1.25x**
- Repeat sentences easily
- Replay target-language audio without penalty

This supports:

- pronunciation development
- listening comprehension
- sound/spelling association
- transition to real speech speed
- optional shadowing

## Shadowing

Shadowing is introduced gradually.

At A0:

- Learners may repeat single words or very short phrases.
- No formal shadowing expectation is required.

At A1 and beyond:

- Learners can repeat sentences aloud.
- Slowed audio can be used first.
- Normal-speed repetition can be introduced later.
- No formal pronunciation instruction is required.

# Course Structure

Each level may include several types of decks.

## 1. Pattern Decks — Core Engine

- Controlled sentences
- High repetition
- Short, predictable structures
- Teach structure implicitly

Example:

```text
I am here.
I am at home.
I am tired.
Are you tired?
```

## 2. Usage Decks — Guided Real-Life Language

- Real-life sentences
- Slight variation
- Reinforce earlier patterns
- Introduce practical vocabulary through context

## 3. Interaction Decks — Conversation Practice

- Back-and-forth structure
- Simple dialogues
- Predictable social situations
- Speaking/listening rhythm

## 4. Vocabulary Expansion Decks — Breadth Without Bloat

These are now an explicit part of the A1/A2 plan.

- Add useful everyday vocabulary
- Keep grammar familiar
- Use short sentences, not word-list memorization
- Introduce new words through repeated practical contexts

Example:

```text
I lost the key.
I found the key.
Do you have the key?
```

## 5. Micro-Story Decks — Controlled Continuity

- 5–12 short cards around one small event
- Recycle vocabulary naturally
- Prepare learners for longer stories at B1

Example:

```text
I can't find my keys.
I look in my bag.
The keys are not there.
I look on the table.
There they are.
```

## 6. Exposure Decks — Larger Input

- Stories, dialogues, adapted native-like material
- Introduced gradually
- Controlled at A2
- Moderate at B1
- Heavy at B2

## 7. Quick Tips Layer — Inline Support

- Short explanations, usually 1–2 sentences
- Added only at confusion points
- No memorization required
- Learners should return immediately to listening and noticing

Quick Tips are now considered a permanent layer across A0–B2, not just an A0 feature.

# Deck Size Guidelines

The current working range is:

| Deck Type | Preferred Size |
|---|---:|
| Focused concept deck | 40–70 cards |
| Standard course deck | 60–90 cards |
| Large beginner foundation deck | 90–120 cards |
| Upper limit for most decks | ~120 cards |

Guidelines:

- A deck like **A0.1 Sound & Meaning Together** can be large because it establishes the sound system and basic meaning.
- A focused deck like **A2.1 Past Tense** can be smaller, but should generally stay above ~40 cards.
- Oversized decks should be split when the themes naturally divide.
- Vocabulary expansion should happen through multiple compact decks, not by bloating existing decks.

# Level Breakdown

## A0 — Bootstrapping

### Goal

By the end of A0, learners should:

- Recognize the sound and rhythm of the language
- Understand simple high-frequency words and phrases
- Understand very short sentences
- Begin noticing repeated patterns
- Participate in tiny conversations

Approximate target:

- **150–250 useful words/chunks**
- **350–500 sentence/card exposures**

### Current Decks

- **A0.1 — Sound & Meaning Together**
- **A0.2 — Core Patterns**
- **A0.3 — Life Around You**
- **A0.4 — Mini Conversations**

### Skills

- Basic comprehension
- Pattern recognition
- Pronunciation familiarity
- Zero grammar knowledge required

### Quick Tip Focus

- Silent letters
- Elision: `j’ai`, `l’eau`, `c’est`
- Liaison/linking sounds
- Gender/article noticing: `le`, `la`, `un`, `une`
- Basic plural noticing when it appears

## A1 — First Real Usage

### Goal

By the end of A1, learners should:

- Handle simple predictable conversations
- Understand familiar everyday sentences
- Ask and answer simple questions
- Use common sentence frames with confidence
- Recognize common connectors and negation

Approximate cumulative target:

- **400–600 useful words/chunks**
- **800–1,100 sentence/card exposures**

### Current Decks

- **A1.1 — Expanded Patterns**
- **A1.2 — Daily Life Situations**
- **A1.3 — Question & Answer Patterns**
- **A1.4 — Conversations Expanded**
- **A1.5 — Polite Questions, Family, Places, and Time**
- **A1.6 — Connectors, Actions, People, and Ideas**

### Notes on A1.5 / A1.6 Split

The original A1.5 Everyday Vocabulary deck was too large for the desired deck-size range. It has been split into two more focused decks:

- **A1.5** focuses on polite questions, family, places, and time.
- **A1.6** focuses on connectors, actions, people, and ideas.

This keeps the course manageable while preserving the sentence-first style.

### Concepts Introduced Implicitly

- Present tense variations
- Basic connectors: and, but, because
- Simple negation
- Family and possession
- Place words and position
- Time expressions
- Polite questions

### Quick Tip Focus

- `ne ... pas`
- `du / de la / de l’`
- `au / aux`
- `mon / ma / mes`, `ton / ta / tes`
- Plurals: written `-s`, usually silent
- Question patterns
- Useful chunks like `est-ce que`, `s’il te plaît`, `s’il vous plaît`

## A2 — Sentence Builder

### Goal

By the end of A2, learners should:

- Handle ordinary daily-life situations
- Understand short connected speech
- Speak in short clusters of sentences
- Recognize past and future patterns
- Express simple opinions, needs, plans, and problems
- Have enough vocabulary breadth to continue into story/dialogue-based B1 work

Approximate cumulative target:

- **750–900 useful words/chunks**
- **1,300–1,600 controlled sentence/card exposures**

The earlier target of ~3,000 sentences is better treated as a later **A0–B1/B2 cumulative exposure goal**, not as an A2 requirement.

### Current Decks

- **A2.1 — Past Tense**
- **A2.2 — Future Tense**
- **A2.3 — Opinions & Reactions**
- **A2.4 — Situational Conversations**
- **A2.5 — Vocabulary Through Micro-Stories**

### Planned A2 Vocabulary Expansion Decks

These decks should add breadth without changing the course philosophy.

- **A2.6 — Everyday Action Verbs**
- **A2.7 — People, Family, and Feelings**
- **A2.8 — Home, Objects, and Chores**
- **A2.9 — Food, Shopping, and Money**
- **A2.10 — City, Transport, and Directions**
- **A2.11 — Time, Routine, and Frequency**
- **A2.12 — Health, Body, and Weather**
- **A2.13 — Problems and Solutions**

Optional if more reinforcement is needed:

- **A2.14 — More Micro-Stories**

### A2 Vocabulary Expansion Rule

Use familiar grammar to introduce new vocabulary.

Good:

```text
I lost the key.
I found the key.
I need the key.
```

Avoid:

```text
A long sentence with new grammar, new vocabulary, and a new idiom all at once.
```

### Concepts Introduced Implicitly

- Common past tense patterns: `j’ai mangé`, `j’ai parlé`, `je suis allé`
- Near future: `je vais manger`
- Opinions: `je pense que...`, `je crois que...`
- Needs: `j’ai besoin de...`
- Obligation: `il faut...`
- Frequency: always, often, sometimes, never
- Everyday problem-solving language

### Quick Tip Focus

- Past tense with `avoir`
- Common `être` past tense patterns
- Near future with `aller + verb`
- Infinitives after `veux`, `peux`, `vais`, `dois`
- Reflexive verbs: `je me réveille`, `je me repose`
- `depuis` for “for/since”
- `chez` for “at/to someone’s place”
- `ni ... ni`
- Adjective agreement: `bon/bonne`, `ouvert/ouverte`, `froid/froide`

# System Rules

## 1. Pattern Before Exposure

| Level | Exposure Type |
|---|---|
| A0 | Almost none; highly controlled cards |
| A1 | Minimal; still controlled |
| A2 | Controlled exposure and micro-stories |
| B1 | Moderate exposure through stories/dialogues |
| B2 | Heavy exposure and native-like material |

---

## 2. Vocabulary Strategy

- No isolated vocabulary memorization as the main method
- Words usually appear in sentences
- Single-word cards are allowed when they support sound/meaning bootstrapping
- High repetition early
- Vocabulary breadth expands deliberately at A1/A2
- Expansion decks should recycle familiar grammar
- Important words should appear multiple times across different contexts
- Useful chunks count as vocabulary

Examples of chunks:

```text
il y a
j’ai besoin de
je viens de
il faut
parce que
est-ce que
s’il te plaît
s’il vous plaît
```

---

## 3. Grammar Approach

Grammar is not taught as a separate subject.

Instead:

- Patterns appear repeatedly.
- Learners notice similarities.
- Quick Tips reduce confusion.
- Formal labels are avoided unless genuinely helpful.

Acceptable:

```text
After je veux, je peux, and je vais, the next verb often stays in its basic form: manger, boire, parler.
```

Avoid:

```text
This is an infinitive complement governed by a semi-auxiliary modal construction.
```

---

## 4. Quick Tips

### Purpose

Reduce confusion without turning the course into grammar study.

### Rules

- 1–2 sentences max when possible
- Plain language
- No memorization required
- Insert at first useful encounter
- Repeat only when helpful
- Do not overwhelm the card

### Recommended Quick Tip Topics

- Silent letters
- Liaison/linking sounds
- Elision: `j’ai`, `l’eau`, `c’est`
- Gender/articles: `le`, `la`, `un`, `une`
- Partitives: `du`, `de la`, `de l’`
- Plurals: written `-s`, usually silent
- Adjective agreement: `bon/bonne`, `ouvert/ouverte`
- Negation: `ne ... pas`, `n’... pas`
- `à + le = au`, `à + les = aux`
- `de + le = du`
- Near future: `je vais + verb`
- Past tense: `j’ai mangé`, `je suis allé`
- Reflexive verbs: `je me réveille`
- Useful chunks: `il faut`, `il y a`, `j’ai besoin de`

---

## 5. Sentence Quality Rules

Sentences should be:

- Short
- Natural
- Useful
- Audio-friendly
- Easy to repeat aloud
- Free of unnecessary grammar load

Avoid sentences that are technically correct but unnatural in ordinary French.

Examples of preferred naturalness fixes:

| Less Natural | Better |
|---|---|
| Elle est ma mère. | C’est ma mère. |
| Je vais ici. | Je viens ici. |
| Je suis allé ici. | Je suis venu ici. |
| Je ne pense pas que c’est bon. | Je pense que ce n’est pas bon. |
| Je mange de la nourriture. | Je mange quelque chose. |
| Il y a du temps. | Il y a encore du temps. |

---

## 6. Counting and Analyzer Strategy

Counting scripts do not need to be perfect to be useful.

However, raw word counts should be treated cautiously because they overcount:

- inflected forms
- plurals
- elided fragments
- title rows
- repeated section labels

A practical analyzer should report:

```text
surface form count
normalized token count
lemma-ish count
phrase/chunk count
words appearing once
words appearing 2–3 times
words appearing 4+ times
```

Recommended normalizations:

```text
j’ → je
c’ → ce
n’ → ne
aujourd’hui should stay one token
parce que should stay one chunk
il y a should stay one chunk
il faut should stay one chunk
s’il te plaît should stay one chunk
s’il vous plaît should stay one chunk
clé / clés → clé
livre / livres → livre
mange / manger / mangé → manger family
parle / parler / parlé → parler family
travaille / travailler / travaillé → travailler family
vais / vas / va / aller / allé → aller family
```

A manual normalization map is preferred over an overly complex NLP pipeline.

## 7. Personalization System

Users can:

- Add sentences from their own life
- Generate translations + audio
- Build custom decks
- Create decks around family, work, hobbies, travel, or routines

This reinforces real-world usage and accelerates learning.

Personalized decks should follow the same rules:

- short sentences
- useful vocabulary
- natural phrasing
- audio-friendly text
- Quick Tips only when helpful

# Design Principles

- Short sentences
- High repetition early
- Variation later
- Minimal explanation
- Real-world usefulness
- Confidence before complexity
- Familiar grammar when introducing new vocabulary
- Compact decks instead of bloated decks
- Audio and replay at the center
- Learners should feel progress quickly

# Notes for New Languages

To reuse this system for another language:

## Adjust

- Sound-based Quick Tips
- Language-specific word order
- Articles/gender/case if relevant
- Common chunks
- Cultural expressions
- Pronunciation challenges
- Writing system challenges if applicable

## Keep

- Deck structure
- Pattern-first progression
- Sentence-first learning
- Audio-first playback
- Quick Tips layer
- Vocabulary expansion through familiar structures
- Gradual transition from cards → conversations → stories → native-like exposure
