# A0 — Foundations

_Status: first draft_  
_Working purpose: prepare the learner for French before the main sentence-building course begins._

## Big Idea

A0 is not where the learner starts “learning French.”

A0 is where the learner stops being shocked by French.

The goal is to make French feel less mysterious before A1 begins. Learners should become familiar with the sound system, spelling patterns, accents, apostrophes, word-linking, and basic article/gender behavior. They will learn useful words along the way, but A0 is not primarily a conversation level or a grammar level.

A0 should feel like:

> “I do not speak French yet, but French no longer looks or sounds completely strange.”

---

## What A0 Is For

By the end of A0, learners should:

- Recognize common French sounds and rhythm.
- Recognize many familiar-looking French words.
- Understand a small set of very common French words.
- Know that accents matter, without needing to explain them.
- Know that apostrophes are normal in French: `j’ai`, `l’eau`, `c’est`.
- Know that French words often link together in speech.
- Know that many nouns come with a small “partner” word: `le`, `la`, `un`, `une`, or `l’`.
- Know how to slow down, repeat, and replay audio in the app.
- Be ready to begin sentence-building in A1.

---

## What A0 Is Not For

A0 should not try to teach full sentence-building.

Remove or move out of A0:

- Core sentence patterns
- “I am / you are / it is” pattern practice
- “Where is...?” / “I want...” / “I have...” sentence-building
- Mini conversations
- Role-play dialogues
- Any expectation that the learner can participate in conversations

The learner may see occasional short phrases or example sentences, but only when they support the main A0 goals: sound, spelling, accents, elision, liaison, or gender/articles.

---

## Revised A0 Goal Statement

By the end of A0, learners should be grounded in the sound and writing behavior of French. They should recognize common sounds, accents, apostrophes, linking, silent letters, and basic articles. They should also know how to use the app’s audio tools effectively.

A0 prepares the learner for A1.

A1 is where the learner begins building sentences and participating in tiny conversations.

---

# A0 Deck Structure

## A0.1 — Cognates: Familiar Words, French Sounds

### Purpose

Use familiar-looking words to make French sound less alien.

Learners should realize:

> “I already recognize many French words. I mainly need to learn how French says them.”

### Design Rules

- No gender yet.
- No article burden yet.
- No sentence-building yet.
- Use audio heavily.
- Use words that are visually familiar to English speakers.
- Choose cognates that cover as many French sounds and spelling patterns as possible.

### Possible Words

Examples only:

```text
action
nation
important
restaurant
hôtel
musique
simple
signal
garage
chocolat
train
menu
café
téléphone
```

### Possible Quick Tips

```text
Tip: Do not pronounce this like English. Listen to the French audio several times.
```

```text
Tip: Try slowing the audio to 0.75x, then replay it until the sound feels familiar.
```

```text
Tip: You are not memorizing spelling rules here. You are building a French sound map.
```

---

## A0.2 — Common French Words: Sound Gaps

### Purpose

Fill in important sounds that cognates do not cover well.

This deck uses very common French words to expose learners to sounds, spellings, and rhythms they need before A1.

### Design Rules

- Use common words.
- Keep the focus on recognition and sound.
- Avoid turning the deck into grammar practice.
- Short phrases are allowed only when they support sound learning.
- Repeat difficult sounds across several words.

### Possible Words

Examples only:

```text
oui
non
je
tu
vous
ça
où
ici
avec
dans
très
bien
mais
pour
bonjour
merci
```

### Sound Gaps to Cover

- French `u`
- `ou`
- `oi`
- nasal sounds: `on`, `an/en`, `in/ain/ein`, `un`
- `r`
- `j` / soft `g`
- `ch`
- silent final letters
- `eu` / `œu`

### Include `œuf`

We should include `œuf` somewhere in A0 because it is common, useful, visually distinctive, and introduces `œ`.

Good candidates:

```text
egg | œuf
an egg | un œuf
```

Possible Quick Tip:

```text
Tip: The symbol `œ` appears in a few common French words, including `œuf`. Just listen and copy the audio.
```

Optional later note, probably not necessary in the first exposure:

```text
Tip: `un œuf` and `des œufs` do not sound exactly the same. Do not worry about that yet — just notice the audio.
```

## A0.3 — Accents and French Characters

### Purpose

Make French accents and special characters familiar, not scary.

Learners should understand:

> “Accents are normal in French. They are not decoration.”

They do not need to memorize accent names, but they should see them, hear examples, and know that they matter.

### Main Items

- Acute: `é`
- Grave: `è`, `à`, `ù`
- Circumflex: `ê`, `â`, `ô`
- Cedilla: `ç`
- Ligature: `œ`

### Design Rules

- Do not turn this into a textbook chapter.
- Use common words whenever possible.
- Give the name of the mark once, then move on.
- Focus on examples and audio.
- Mention typing French characters, but do not over-explain.

### Possible Cards

```text
coffee | café
summer | été
very | très
there | là
where | où
that | ça
boy | garçon
hotel | hôtel
egg | œuf
sister | sœur
heart | cœur
```

### Possible Quick Tips

```text
Tip: `é` is called e acute. You do not need to memorize the name yet — just notice the mark and listen.
```

```text
Tip: Accents are not decoration. Sometimes they change the sound, and sometimes they distinguish two different words.
```

```text
Tip: You will eventually need characters like `é`, `è`, `à`, `ç`, `ù`, and `œ`. Set up a French keyboard or learn how to type accented characters on your device.
```

```text
Tip: `ç` keeps the c soft, like an English s sound.
```

## A0.4 — Elision: Apostrophes Are Normal

### Purpose

Show why French words often look “crammed together.”

Learners should understand:

> “French often drops a vowel before another vowel. The apostrophe shows where that happened.”

This is not a grammar lesson. It is an orientation deck.

### Design Rules

- Keep the explanation extremely short.
- Use common examples.
- Let audio do most of the teaching.
- Include before/after examples when helpful.
- Avoid rare or complex cases.

### Core Examples

```text
je ai → j’ai
le ami → l’ami
la eau → l’eau
ce est → c’est
ne est pas → n’est pas
```

### Possible Cards

```text
I have | j’ai
the water | l’eau
the friend | l’ami
it is / this is | c’est
it is not | ce n’est pas
```

### Possible Quick Tips

```text
Tip: French often drops a vowel before another vowel. That is why `je ai` becomes `j’ai`.
```

```text
Tip: The apostrophe is normal. It usually means two words have been pulled together in speech and writing.
```

```text
Tip: Do not try to pronounce both vowels separately. Copy the audio.
```

---

## A0.5 — Liaison: French Links Words

### Purpose

Show that some normally silent final letters are pronounced before a vowel.

Learners should understand:

> “French likes to flow. Sometimes a silent final letter wakes up when the next word begins with a vowel.”

### Design Rules

- Teach only common, high-value cases.
- Do not require memorization of liaison rules.
- Use audio-first examples.
- Avoid explaining all exceptions.
- Keep this as noticing, not mastery.

### Possible Examples

```text
les amis
vous avez
nous avons
deux ans
mon ami
un petit enfant
```

### Possible Quick Tips

```text
Tip: Sometimes a final silent letter is pronounced before a vowel. This helps French flow.
```

```text
Tip: In `les amis`, the silent `s` links forward and sounds like `z`.
```

```text
Tip: You do not need to master liaison now. Just notice that French words often connect in speech.
```

## A0.6 — Gender and Articles: The Word Comes with a Little Partner

### Purpose

Introduce the idea that French nouns often travel with article words:

- `le`
- `la`
- `l’`
- `un`
- `une`

Learners should understand:

> “In French, it is useful to learn a noun together with its article.”

This deck should reuse words from the cognates and common-words decks so the learner is not dealing with too much new sound and meaning at once.

### Design Rules

- Reuse familiar words.
- Introduce the word alone first when possible.
- Then show the word with `the`.
- Then show the word with `a/an`.
- Avoid heavy grammar terminology.
- Avoid explaining every gender rule.
- Let repetition do most of the work.

### Preferred Card Style

```text
explosion | explosion
the explosion | l’explosion
an explosion | une explosion
```

```text
restaurant | restaurant
the restaurant | le restaurant
a restaurant | un restaurant
```

```text
music | musique
the music | la musique
```

```text
egg | œuf
the egg | l’œuf
an egg | un œuf
```

### Possible Quick Tips

```text
Tip: French nouns often come with a small partner word like `le`, `la`, `un`, or `une`.
```

```text
Tip: Do not worry about memorizing gender rules yet. Learn the article with the word.
```

```text
Tip: Before a vowel, `le` and `la` often become `l’`, as in `l’explosion`.
```

# App Guidance Inside A0

A0 should teach app usage in context rather than through a full separate “how to use the app” deck.

## Audio Speed

Use occasional hints:

```text
Tip: If this sounds too fast, slow the audio to 0.75x or 0.50x.
```

```text
Tip: Try this at 0.75x first, then replay it at normal speed.
```

## Replay

```text
Tip: Replay the French audio several times. Your goal is to get the sound in your head.
```

```text
Tip: Do not rush. Repeating one clear sound five times is useful.
```

## Passive / Autoplay

```text
Tip: Passive mode is useful after you have seen the cards once. Let the deck play while you listen.
```

## Definitions and Usage Examples

```text
Tip: Double-tap a word to see its definition and examples in other sentences.
```

## French Keyboard

Include a short note in the A0 document or a dismissible hint:

```text
You will eventually need French characters like `é`, `è`, `à`, `ç`, `ù`, and `œ`. Set up a French keyboard, or learn how to long-press letters on your phone.
```

No detailed setup instructions are needed inside the deck.

# A0 Quick Tip Style Guide

Quick Tips should be:

- short
- reassuring
- directly connected to the current card
- audio-focused
- not grammar-heavy
- easy to ignore if the learner already understands

Good:

```text
Tip: The apostrophe is normal. French often drops a vowel before another vowel.
```

Avoid:

```text
Elision is the obligatory deletion of a final unstressed vowel before a following vowel or mute h in certain grammatical environments.
```

Good:

```text
Tip: `ç` sounds like s. Listen to `ça`.
```

Avoid:

```text
The cedilla is an orthographic diacritic used to indicate palatalization or softening of the grapheme c before back vowels.
```

# Final A0 Design Principle

A0 is a preparation level.

It does not try to teach the learner to speak French yet. It teaches the learner how French sounds, how French writing behaves, how words connect, and how to use the app effectively before beginning sentence-building in A1.

The learner should leave A0 thinking:

> “French still feels new, but it no longer feels random.”
